What is Coronary Artery Disease (CAD)?

Coronary Artery Disease (CAD) is the most common type of heart disease. It occurs when the arteries that supply blood to the heart muscle become hardened and narrowed due to buildup of cholesterol and other materials, called plaque, on their inner walls. This buildup is called atherosclerosis.

As plaque builds up, the arteries narrow over time, reducing blood flow to the heart. This can lead to chest pain (angina), shortness of breath, or even a heart attack if a plaque ruptures and completely blocks blood flow.

Key Facts:
  • CAD is the leading cause of death worldwide
  • Early detection and management can significantly reduce risks
  • Many risk factors are modifiable through lifestyle changes
  • Regular screening is recommended for at-risk individuals

Risk Factors for CAD

Modifiable Risk Factors
High Blood Pressure High Cholesterol Smoking Diabetes Obesity Physical Inactivity Unhealthy Diet Stress

Impact: These factors can be controlled through lifestyle changes and medication, significantly reducing CAD risk.

Non-Modifiable Risk Factors
Age (≥45 for men, ≥55 for women) Family History Gender (Male) Race/Ethnicity

Impact: While these cannot be changed, awareness helps guide screening frequency and preventive measures.

Protective Factors
Regular Exercise Healthy Diet Normal BMI No Smoking Stress Management Regular Check-ups

Impact: Incorporating these factors can significantly reduce CAD risk and improve overall health.

Prevention Strategies

Lifestyle Modifications
  • 150 minutes of moderate exercise weekly
  • Heart-healthy Mediterranean diet
  • Maintain healthy weight (BMI 18.5-24.9)
  • Limit alcohol consumption
  • Smoking cessation
  • Stress reduction techniques
Medical Management
  • Regular blood pressure monitoring
  • Cholesterol screening every 4-6 years
  • Diabetes screening if at risk
  • Aspirin therapy if indicated
  • Statins for high cholesterol
  • Blood pressure medications if needed

Frequently Asked Questions

What are the early warning signs?

Early signs may include chest discomfort (angina), shortness of breath, fatigue with exertion, and palpitations. However, some people may have no symptoms until a heart attack occurs.

How often should I be screened?

Adults should have their cholesterol checked every 4-6 years starting at age 20. More frequent screening is recommended if you have risk factors or abnormal results.

Can CAD be reversed?

While CAD cannot be completely reversed, aggressive lifestyle changes and medications can slow progression and sometimes partially reverse plaque buildup.

What's a normal blood pressure?

Normal blood pressure is less than 120/80 mmHg. Elevated blood pressure (120-129/<80) increases risk and should be monitored.

How accurate is this risk prediction tool?

Our tool uses validated algorithms based on Framingham Heart Study data. While highly predictive, it's for educational purposes and should be used alongside clinical judgment.

What should I do with high-risk results?

High-risk results warrant immediate consultation with a cardiologist for comprehensive evaluation and management planning.
